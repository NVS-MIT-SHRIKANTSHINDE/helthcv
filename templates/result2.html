Skin Disease Image Classification

AK (Actinic Keratosis)

A precancerous area of thick, scaly, or crusty skin caused by sun damage. May develop into squamous cell carcinoma if untreated.

BCC (Basal Cell Carcinoma)

The most common type of skin cancer, presenting as a pearly bump or a red patch. It grows slowly and rarely spreads to other parts of the body.

BKL (Benign Keratosis-like Lesion)

Non-cancerous skin growths often found on the face, chest, or back. These lesions are typically harmless but can be mistaken for skin cancer.

DF (Dermatofibroma)

A benign tumor of the skin, often appearing as a small, firm, brown or pink bump. It is generally harmless but can be mistaken for other skin conditions.

MEL (Melanoma)

A dangerous type of skin cancer that can develop from a mole or appear suddenly. It has irregular borders, multiple colors, and can spread to other parts of the body.

NV (Nevi)

Commonly known as moles, these are benign growths of melanocytes. Most are harmless, but some can develop into melanoma.

SCC (Squamous Cell Carcinoma)

A type of skin cancer that typically appears as a red, scaly patch or a firm bump. It can spread to other parts of the body if not treated promptly.

VASC (Vascular Lesion)

Lesions caused by abnormalities in blood vessels. These can appear as red or purple spots and are typically benign, though some may require treatment.
